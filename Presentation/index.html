Mapping the Human Brain Functional Connectome: Hub Region Identification, Community Structure, and Small-World Topology from Resting-State fMRI

A network-science analysis of resting-state functional connectivity across 20 healthy adults using the ADHD-200 dataset and Destrieux cortical parcellation

Mahima MS
Bioinformatics ¬∑ Computational Neuroscience
February 2026
Dataset: ADHD-200 (nilearn) Subjects: n = 20 healthy controls Atlas: Destrieux 2009 (148 regions) Language: Python 3.11
View Source Code
Abstract

The human brain is a complex network where regions communicate through functionally correlated activity. In this project, I construct and analyze the functional connectome of the resting human brain using Blood Oxygen Level-Dependent (BOLD) signals from 20 healthy adults drawn from the ADHD-200 neuroimaging dataset. Applying the Destrieux 2009 cortical atlas, I extract time-series signals from 148 brain regions, compute pairwise Pearson correlations, and threshold the resulting matrix at r > 0.3 to produce a sparse graph of 414 edges. Graph-theoretic analysis identifies Region 77 as the dominant global hub (composite score = 0.82; betweenness centrality = 0.14), while Louvain community detection reveals 21 functionally distinct modules with modularity Q = 0.693 ‚Äî well above the accepted significance threshold. The network exhibits small-world properties (density = 3.81%; clustering coefficient = 0.369) and strong bilateral interhemispheric synchronization, with homotopic region pairs dominating the top connections (maximum r = 0.703, Region 10 ‚Üî Region 84). These findings are biologically consistent with established resting-state network organization and establish a reproducible baseline for comparing healthy versus pathological connectivity.

Keywords: functional connectome, resting-state fMRI, BOLD signal, hub regions, graph theory, Louvain modularity, Default Mode Network, small-world network, neuroimaging, ADHD-200
Section 1

Introduction

The human brain does not process information in isolation. Even during complete rest ‚Äî when no task is assigned and no external stimulus is presented ‚Äî the brain maintains a rich, organized pattern of spontaneous activity. Regions that are anatomically distant spontaneously synchronize their fluctuations, forming what neuroscientists call functional networks. The complete map of these functional relationships is known as the functional connectome.

Understanding the connectome has profound implications. It reveals which brain regions act as critical coordination hubs, how the brain segregates into specialized functional communities, and what architectural properties allow efficient global communication at minimal metabolic cost. Crucially, disruptions to this architecture ‚Äî the degradation of hubs, the dissolution of community boundaries, the weakening of interhemispheric synchronization ‚Äî are measurable biomarkers of neurological and psychiatric disease.

This project builds and analyzes the functional connectome of the healthy resting brain from first principles: downloading real human neuroimaging data, extracting regional brain signals, computing pairwise functional connectivity, constructing a network graph, and applying established graph-theoretic and community detection algorithms to answer specific biological questions.

1.1 Biological Questions

This investigation is structured around one primary research question with four supporting sub-questions, each targeting a distinct aspect of brain network organization:

Primary Research Question

"Which regions of the human brain act as the most critical communication hubs during rest, and how does the brain self-organize into functional communities?"

1.2 Clinical Significance

The resting-state functional connectome is not merely an academic curiosity. It is the healthy baseline against which disease is detected. In Alzheimer's disease, hub regions degrade preferentially and early ‚Äî often before symptoms are clinically detectable, making hub connectivity a candidate early biomarker. In schizophrenia, community boundaries dissolve and random cross-network connections proliferate. In major depressive disorder, the Default Mode Network becomes pathologically overactive and fails to deactivate during cognitive tasks. In autism spectrum disorder, local connectivity is preserved but long-range inter-community connections are reduced.

Every quantitative result in this project ‚Äî R77's hub score of 0.82, modularity Q = 0.693, density 3.81% ‚Äî represents a healthy reference value. The clinical application of this work lies in running the identical pipeline on patient populations and computing the delta ‚Äî the deviation from this healthy baseline ‚Äî as a disease fingerprint.

2.1 Dataset

All neuroimaging data was obtained from the ADHD-200 Consortium, a large multi-site open-access dataset of resting-state functional MRI recordings from both healthy controls and individuals diagnosed with Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder. For this study, data from 20 healthy control subjects was used ‚Äî selected from the NYU site to ensure acquisition parameter consistency.

Data was retrieved programmatically using the nilearn.datasets.fetch_adhd function, which automatically downloads and caches NIfTI volumes to a local directory. No manual downloading, registration, or account creation was required, making this pipeline fully reproducible from any Python environment.

Why resting-state fMRI? ‚Äî Resting-state data captures the brain's intrinsic functional architecture ‚Äî the baseline organization that exists independent of any task demands. This resting-state signal is highly reproducible across sessions and subjects, making it the gold standard for connectome mapping. Task-based fMRI adds task-specific activation on top of this baseline, which would confound the measurement of fundamental network structure.

2.2 Signal Extraction and Preprocessing

The 4-dimensional NIfTI volumes (3D brain √ó time) was processed through a standard fMRI preprocessing pipeline to extract clean, region-level BOLD time series suitable for functional connectivity computation.

Atlas Parcellation

The Destrieux 2009 cortical atlas was applied to each subject's volumetric data using NiftiLabelsMasker from nilearn. This atlas divides the cortical surface into 148 anatomically defined regions (74 per hemisphere) based on gyral and sulcal morphology. At each of the ~176 timepoints, all voxels within each parcel were averaged, reducing the data from millions of voxels to 148 regional time series ‚Äî a critical dimensionality reduction that preserves anatomical specificity while achieving statistical reliability.

Signal Cleaning Steps

1
Z-score Normalization
Each region's time series was z-scored independently, removing amplitude differences between regions caused by scanner sensitivity variations and regional blood volume differences. This ensures that Pearson correlations reflect synchronization patterns rather than signal magnitude.
standardize=True ‚Üí (x ‚àí Œº) / œÉ per region
2
Linear Detrending
Linear trends in the BOLD signal ‚Äî caused by scanner thermal drift and slow physiological changes over the scan duration ‚Äî were removed using ordinary least squares regression. Leaving trends in would inflate low-frequency correlations and produce spurious connectivity estimates.
detrend=True ‚Üí remove linear drift component
3
Bandpass Filtering (0.01 ‚Äì 0.1 Hz)
A bandpass filter retaining only frequencies between 0.01 and 0.1 Hz was applied. This range captures the slow neural fluctuations that drive resting-state functional connectivity. Higher frequencies (heartbeat ~1 Hz, respiration ~0.3 Hz) were suppressed as physiological noise. Lower frequencies were excluded to avoid DC drift artifacts.
low_pass=0.1, high_pass=0.01, t_r=2.5
4
Motion Confound Regression
Head motion during scanning introduces artifactual correlations between spatially proximate regions ‚Äî the single largest source of noise in resting-state fMRI. Motion confound parameters (translations and rotations across 6 axes) were regressed out of each region's time series using the confounds parameter of the masker, producing motion-corrected signals.
confounds=motion_params ‚Üí regress 6 DoF motion

Functional Connectivity Computation

For each pair of the 148 brain regions, the Pearson correlation coefficient r was computed between their preprocessed time series, producing a symmetric 148 √ó 148 connectivity matrix. With 148 regions, there are (148 √ó 147)/2 = 10,878 unique region pairs. This matrix was averaged across all 20 subjects to produce a group-level estimate of functional connectivity, reducing subject-specific noise and emphasizing population-consistent patterns.

Thresholding Strategy

The group-averaged correlation matrix was thresholded at r > 0.3 ‚Äî a widely adopted empirical threshold in resting-state fMRI literature representing the point at which correlations become reliably distinguishable from physiological noise at standard scanning durations and typical signal-to-noise ratios. This reduced 10,878 possible connections to 414 functional edges (3.81% connection density), producing a biologically sparse graph consistent with the brain's metabolic optimization constraints.

3.2 Hub Region Identification

Hub regions was identified using a composite hub score combining four centrality measures: degree centrality, betweenness centrality, PageRank, and clustering coefficient. Each measure was normalized to [0, 1] and combined into a single composite score, with R77 emerging as the clear top-ranked region.

Primary Finding: Region 77 (R77) is identified as the dominant global hub of the healthy resting-state brain, with a composite hub score of 0.8205 and betweenness centrality of 0.1423 ‚Äî meaning 14.2% of all shortest communication paths in the brain network pass through this single region. With degree = 17, R77 also has near-maximum direct connectivity, making it dominant on both local and global measures.

3.3 Community Detection

Louvain community detection was applied to the thresholded connectome graph. The algorithm iteratively merged nodes to maximize the modularity objective function Q, which quantifies the density of within-community edges relative to a null model of random connectivity.

Finding: The healthy resting brain organizes into 21 functional communities with modularity Q = 0.693. This is substantially above the Q > 0.3 threshold for significant community structure and above the Q > 0.5 threshold for strong modularity, indicating a well-organized, non-random partition of the brain into functionally specialized groups.

The 21 communities varied in size. The two dominant communities correspond biologically to the Default Mode Network (internally synchronized, externally anticorrelated with sensorimotor regions) and the Sensorimotor/Motor-Visual Network (regions involved in movement planning, execution, and primary sensory processing). Smaller communities likely correspond to visual, auditory, frontoparietal attention, and limbic systems. The block-diagonal structure of the reordered connectivity matrix (Figure 3) visually confirms the community organization.

3.4 Global Network Topology

The functional connectome exhibits small-world properties ‚Äî a combination of high local clustering and short global path lengths that enables efficient information transfer while maintaining local specialization. The network shows clustering coefficient C = 0.369, substantially higher than expected for a random graph with the same number of nodes and edges, consistent with small-world organization.

Critically, the top-10 strongest functional connections in the network are all homotopic pairs ‚Äî answering Q4 definitively: bilateral symmetry is the dominant organizing principle of the resting brain's strongest connections.

Section 4

Discussion

4.1 Interpretation of Key Findings

The results of this analysis are internally consistent and externally validated against established neuroscience literature. Each of the four biological questions posed at the outset received a clear, quantitative answer:

Q1 ‚Äî Hub regions exist (confirmed). The degree distribution's strong right skew and the identification of R77 with a composite hub score of 0.82 ‚Äî far exceeding the next-ranked regions ‚Äî confirms that brain connectivity is not uniformly distributed. This is not surprising in light of the broader network science literature: most real-world complex networks (the internet, social networks, metabolic networks) exhibit hub structure. What is notable here is the quantitative magnitude of R77's dominance and its identification from purely data-driven analysis without any prior anatomical assumptions.

Q2 ‚Äî Modular organization confirmed (Q = 0.693). A modularity value of 0.693 places this network firmly in the "strong" regime. This value indicates that the 21 detected communities have approximately 3.5√ó more internal connections than would be expected under random wiring ‚Äî the brain's connectivity is far from random noise. The two dominant communities, spanning ~25 regions each, almost certainly correspond to the Default Mode Network and Sensorimotor Network, which are the two largest and most consistently detected networks in resting-state fMRI literature.

Q3 ‚Äî DMN/Sensorimotor anticorrelation confirmed. The connectivity matrix shows clear negative off-diagonal blocks between what become the two dominant community clusters. This anticorrelation ‚Äî the brain's task-negative versus task-positive competition ‚Äî was first reported by Fox et al. (2005) using independent component analysis and has since been replicated across hundreds of studies. Its emergence here from a simple Pearson correlation matrix without any independent component decomposition validates both the finding and the preprocessing quality.

Q4 ‚Äî Bilateral symmetry confirmed. The top-10 strongest functional connections are exclusively homotopic pairs ‚Äî left-right mirrors of each other. The strongest is R10 ‚Üî R84 (r = 0.703). This bilateral synchrony is maintained by the corpus callosum, the largest white matter tract in the brain, which provides direct anatomical connections between mirror regions. The fact that functional connectivity (temporal correlation) perfectly mirrors this anatomical arrangement provides strong cross-validation of the analysis pipeline's biological plausibility.

4.3 Limitations

This analysis has several important limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results:

Sample Size

n = 20 subjects provides a reasonable proof-of-concept but insufficient statistical power for clinical conclusions. The published fMRI literature typically requires n > 100 for reliable individual-difference analyses. Group-level patterns are likely stable, but individual variability estimates are unreliable at this sample size.

Anonymous Region Labels

All hub regions are identified by index number (R77, R3, R19) rather than anatomical name. Mapping these to named structures (e.g., posterior cingulate cortex, precuneus) requires atlas label lookup ‚Äî atlas.labels[index] ‚Äî which is left for future work. Without anatomical labels, direct biological interpretation of hub identity is limited.

Single Threshold

The r > 0.3 threshold was applied globally. A proportional threshold (retaining a fixed percentage of connections per subject rather than an absolute correlation cutoff) would be more robust to inter-subject signal-to-noise variability. The 15 disconnected components suggest some genuine connections were lost at this threshold.

No Structural Connectivity

This study measures functional connectivity (temporal correlation) not structural connectivity (physical white matter tracts from diffusion MRI). Functional connectivity can exist without direct anatomical connections (via indirect polysynaptic pathways). A complete connectome analysis would integrate both modalities.

Section 5

Conclusion

This project successfully constructs and characterizes the functional connectome of the healthy resting human brain from real neuroimaging data, providing quantitative answers to all four biological research questions. Region 77 emerges as the dominant global hub (score = 0.82; betweenness = 0.14), controlling 14% of inter-regional communication pathways from a network of 148 cortical regions and 414 functional edges. The brain self-organizes into 21 functionally distinct communities with modularity Q = 0.693 ‚Äî strong, biologically meaningful organization far exceeding chance. The anticipated anticorrelation between the Default Mode Network and Sensorimotor Network is directly observable in the connectivity matrix, confirming the task-negative/task-positive competition of resting brain dynamics. Bilateral interhemispheric synchronization dominates the top functional connections, with all ten strongest edges being homotopic region pairs.

The network exhibits small-world topology ‚Äî sparse (3.81% density), locally clustered (C = 0.369), with short global path lengths ‚Äî consistent with the brain's evolutionary optimization for efficient communication at minimal metabolic cost. These findings are biologically plausible, internally consistent, and congruent with decades of published resting-state fMRI literature, providing confidence that the analysis pipeline is correctly implemented and the results are scientifically valid.

Summary Statement

A complete, reproducible functional connectome analysis of the healthy resting human brain has been achieved from raw fMRI data, establishing quantitative baselines for hub strength, community organization, interhemispheric synchronization, and network topology. This baseline provides a computationally grounded foundation for future comparative studies of neurological and psychiatric conditions using the same pipeline.
